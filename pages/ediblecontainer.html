Edible containers

Food containers made from tempeh

Biodesign challenge 2021

Finding an alternative against single-use plastics food containers used on festivals.

In this year's Minor we will be participating in the *Biodesign challenge to use living organisms such as mycelium to tackle a problem and create new alternatives or solutions in this case edible containers.

*Biodesign Challenge is an education program and competition that is shaping the first generation of biodesigners. BDC partners high school and university students with scientists, artists, and designers to envision, create, and critique transformational applications in biotech.

Framing: Design from another angle

The issue we came across was the way festivals use non biodegradable materials for different types of consumptions. Such as cups, cutlery and even plastic wraps for food. Kwaku festival is one of these festivals where food and coming together is the main goal. Not only is this culture very present here but also the single-use containers used carry your food in.

With this in mind and the kwaku festival which is also our community we focus on. Since we are both surinamese we want to give back to our community and better the world not by only lowering single-use plastics on festivals such as kwaku but also give an example to the rest of the world that there are other more sustainable and environmentally friendly ways.

With our community in mind and the knowledge of finding something that is already accepted in our community we have a steady motivation to find ideas that fit well into this picture.

This brings up the question; Is there a way we could introduce a new product as an alternative (not a replacement) for these non biodegradable materials (like plastic)?

Surinamese culture and tempeh

Inspired by our own culture we see opportunities in the way tempeh is integrated in the already delicious food made by our own families and communities, this is the reason why tempeh is the best candidate for making our own edible container. Because the threshold to eat tempeh as part of the meal already has connections that is perceived as positive in the culture, by making the link to edible containers made from tempeh more easily accepted. Thus the integration will go more smoothly and fits so well into the menu.
